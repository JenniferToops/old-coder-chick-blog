These aren't fairytales, and I'm not your fairy godmother...

Setting up my workspace

It took two years for me to figure out that I needed a desktop with two monitors, a webcam, and speakers. Like other coding newbies, I didn't have a ton of money to spend, so I started my foray into coding by watching YouTube tutorials and coding in Notepad (yes, Notepad, not Notepad++) and in the console of the laptop I had bought when I was married.

My relationship with coding was an on-again-off-again, turbulent affair. I became frustrated watching coding tutorials on half of the 15" screen and coding in tiny font on the other half. Sometimes, I used my kids' chromebooks to watch the videos while I coded in my laptop. I had real estate files next to coding tutorials, and everything was a jumbled mess. After a quick reality check (and disappointing glance at my bank account), I decided that I likely would not be running complex calculations, I wasn't doing much with graphics and animation, and I was starting a bootcamp in the middle of a pandemic... in other words, coffee shop coding wasn't on my radar. No sleek, flashy Apple products for this girl. What I really needed was a decent, used desktop built like a tank and as fast as a racehorse. With more apprehension than anticipation, I ordered the "brains" of my tank and hoped for the best.

I'm glad that my expectations were low because my initial impression of Tank was not great. The seller had stuffed Tank into a large garbage bag, then placed that bag into another bag and filled the outer bag with spray insulation--you know, the stuff they put behind walls in houses. They boxed, labeled, and shipped that mess to yours truly. When I finally peeled of all the layers of insulation, I discovered what could best be described as the poster child of a scratch-&-dent ad.

My beat-up old Tank has carried me through two (almost three--that's another story for another blog) real estate transactions as well as six months of a coding bootcamp. I have no regrets about my purchase and am proud of the fact I am doing the same work on my beat-up workstation as other people who spent three times as much on their flashy systems. Most of my peripherals came from Facebook Marketplace or Amazon's Lightning Deals. The life of a single mom is bottom-lining almost everything, focusing on functionality, value, and features--a mindset that seems to mesh well with the software engineering industry.
